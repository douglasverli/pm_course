### Video 2: Core Management Functions
**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Learning Objectives:**
- Master the four core management functions
- Understand how they interconnect
- Apply them to real situations

---

**SCRIPT:**

[SETTLE INTO TEACHING POSITION]

Okay, so let's talk about what management actually does. There are four core functions, and they're kind of like the four wheels on a car – you need all of them working properly, or the whole thing doesn't go anywhere.

The first one is **Planning**. 

Planning is where you figure out where you're going and how you're going to get there. It's not just "let's make more money" – though that's part of it. It's about setting specific, measurable goals. It's about understanding your market. It's about anticipating challenges. It's about thinking a few steps ahead.

Think about it like this: If you're planning a road trip, you don't just get in the car and hope for the best, right? You figure out your destination, you check the route, you think about what you'll need, you maybe plan where to stop. That's planning.

In business, this might look like: "We want to increase our market share by 15% in the next two years. Here's how we'll do it – we'll expand into three new regions, we'll hire this many people, we'll invest in this technology." That's planning.

I've seen companies that skip this step, and let me tell you, they end up reacting to everything instead of leading anything. They're always putting out fires instead of building something.

The second function is **Organizing**.

Once you know where you're going, you've got to organize the resources to get there. This is about structure. It's about deciding who does what, what departments you need, how information flows. It's about putting the right people in the right roles.

Organizing answers questions like: Who reports to whom? What's each person responsible for? Do we organize by product, by region, by function? How do we make sure people have what they need to do their jobs? How do we avoid silos where departments don't talk to each other?

Here's a practical example: Let's say you run a growing e-commerce company. You started with just you and two other people doing everything. But now you've got 50 employees. You can't have everyone reporting directly to you – that's chaos. So you organize. Maybe you create a sales department, an operations department, a customer service department. Each has a manager. Information flows in specific ways. Decisions can be made faster because people know who's responsible for what.

The third function is **Leading**.

This is where the human element really comes in. Leading is about motivating people, guiding them, setting the tone for the organization. It's about communication and influence. It's about inspiring people to care about what they're doing.

A lot of people confuse managing and leading, and honestly, they're different. You can manage without leading – you can tell people what to do and track whether they did it. But that's not going to get you extraordinary results. That's going to get you minimum compliance.

Leading is about getting people to want to do things, about creating a vision that people actually care about. It's about building trust. It's about being the kind of leader people want to follow, not the kind they have to follow.

Think about leaders you've admired. They probably had some quality that made you want to follow them, made you want to do good work for them. Maybe they believed in you when you didn't believe in yourself. Maybe they made the vision clear and inspiring. Maybe they treated people with respect. That's leadership.

The last function is **Controlling**.

Now, "controlling" sounds a bit harsh, but it's really about monitoring and feedback. It's about checking: Are we on track? Are we actually achieving what we planned? If we're not, what do we need to change?

Controlling includes measuring performance, giving feedback, and making adjustments. If you plan to increase sales by 15%, but three months in you're only at 5%, you notice that. You analyze why. You adjust.

And here's the thing – this isn't about punishing people for missing targets. Good controlling is about course-correction. Something's not working, so we figure out what and fix it.

Here's the thing though – these aren't separate activities that happen in sequence. They're all happening at the same time, and they all feed into each other.

You plan, then as you're executing that plan, you're leading people, you've organized them into roles, and you're constantly monitoring – checking progress, getting feedback, adjusting as needed. But that feedback from monitoring might cause you to revise your plan. And maybe you need to reorganize.

So a good manager is doing all four of these things simultaneously, constantly balancing them. It's like juggling, except the balls are interconnected.

### Discussion Points:

1. **Think about an organization you're familiar with.** How do you see these four functions playing out? Does it feel balanced, or is one missing?

2. **What happens when a company is good at planning and organizing but weak on leading?** What about the opposite?

3. **In your own work, which of these functions do you think is most challenging?** Which comes naturally to you?
