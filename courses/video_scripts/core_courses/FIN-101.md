# FIN-101: Introduction to Finance
## Video Script: "Finance Is Just Decisions About Money"
**Instructor:** Douglas Verli

### Video 1: What Is Finance, Really?
**Duration:** 14 minutes
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand what finance actually is
- See how finance affects your life
- Get introduced to the major topics we'll cover

---

**SCRIPT:**

[LEAN INTO CAMERA WITH GENUINE TONE]

Hey everyone, I'm Douglas Verli. Welcome to Introduction to Finance.

Let me start with something real: How many of you think finance is boring? Yeah, I see those hands in my mind. Here's the thing – finance seems boring when you think of it as spreadsheets and decimal points and compound interest formulas. But when you reframe it, finance is actually about decisions. Decisions about money. And decisions about money? That affects your life constantly.

Think about the last purchase you made. A coffee, a book, whatever. You made a financial decision. You had some money, you could spend it on that or on something else, and you chose. That's finance. That's financial decision-making.

Or think bigger: If you're planning to buy a house someday, you need to understand mortgages, interest rates, whether it's a good time to buy. If you're thinking about investing in your future, you need to understand stocks, bonds, risk, diversification. If you run a business, you need to know if you're actually making money or just fooling yourself. That's all finance.

So here's what finance really is: It's the science and art of managing money. It's about understanding the time value of money – the fact that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because of inflation and because you can invest it. It's about understanding risk. It's about making informed decisions with incomplete information. It's about optimizing.

In this course, we're going to cover several big areas:

First, we'll talk about **financial statements**. These are how companies communicate their financial health. If you understand how to read them, you can tell if a company is actually doing well or if it's just pretending. These statements – the income statement, the balance sheet, the cash flow statement – they tell you everything.

Second, **the fundamentals of investing**. We'll talk about stocks, bonds, mutual funds. What they are, how they work, what the risks are. We'll talk about building a portfolio – a collection of investments that's appropriate for you. We'll talk about risk and return.

Third, **personal finance**. This is practical stuff. How to think about debt, budgeting, saving for retirement. How to not make dumb financial decisions. How to build wealth over time.

Fourth, **business finance**. If you're running a company or thinking of starting one, you need to understand cash flow, capital structure, how to make investment decisions. You need to understand how to keep your business alive.

We'll also talk about **financial markets** – what they are, how they work, why they sometimes seem to behave irrationally. We'll talk about economics basics, because the macro environment affects your finances.

Now, here's what's important to understand from the get-go: Finance has a lot of formulas and mathematics. But the math is just a tool. The real skill is understanding what the formulas mean and knowing which questions to ask.

A lot of people think finance is about predicting the future perfectly. It's not. Markets are influenced by human emotion, unexpected events, new information. What finance is really about is understanding probabilities, understanding risks, making decisions despite uncertainty.

So here's my goal for you: By the end of this course, you're going to understand the financial world better. You're going to be able to read a financial statement and understand what it's telling you. You're going to be able to make smarter personal financial decisions. You're going to understand why interest rates matter, why the stock market moves the way it does. And if you're interested in business, you'll understand how companies think about financial decisions.

Now, a warning: This course involves numbers. If you haven't done math in a while, don't panic. We're going to go slowly. And honestly, if you can do basic algebra, you can do this. This isn't about being a math genius. It's about understanding concepts.

Here's what I want from you: Think of finance as a language. In the next few weeks, you're going to learn to speak this language. And once you do, you'll understand conversations happening around you that you couldn't understand before. You'll make better decisions with your money. That matters.

You ready?

### Video 2: The Money Mindset
**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand how to think about money
- Learn the difference between earning, saving, and investing
- Get perspective on financial goals

---

**SCRIPT:**

[SETTLE IN, SPEAK FROM EXPERIENCE]

Before we get into the technical stuff, I want to talk about the mindset around money.

Money isn't good or bad. It's a tool. It's a medium of exchange – it lets you do things. You can buy food, you can travel, you can support causes you care about. But a lot of people have weird relationships with money. Some people are afraid of it. Some people think wanting money is greedy or shallow. Some people just never think about it and then are surprised when they're broke.

Let me clear something up: It's okay to want to have money. It's okay to want to improve your financial situation. That's not greedy, that's just smart. That's just wanting to have options and security and the ability to do things that matter to you.

So here's how I want you to think about your financial life: There are three things happening.

First, you're **earning** money. You work, you get paid. For some of you, that might be a salary. For some, it might be a business. For some, it might be multiple income streams. But somehow, money is coming in.

Second, you're **spending** money. You have to live – you need food, shelter, maybe transportation. But beyond necessities, you spend money on things you want. That's fine. Humans aren't robots. You should enjoy your life. Money is a tool for living, not something to hoard.

Third, if there's money left over after you spend it, you can **save** it or **invest** it.

Now here's where it gets interesting: Most people focus only on earning. "I need to make more money." And yeah, making more is helpful. But here's the reality – the difference between people who become wealthy and people who don't isn't usually their income. It's what they do with it.

I've known people making six figures who are broke, and I've known people making $50,000 a year who are building real wealth. The difference? What they do with what they earn.

If you make $100,000 a year but spend $110,000, you're going to be broke and in debt no matter what. You're going backwards. If you make $60,000 but spend $40,000 and invest $20,000, you're going to build wealth.

So spending less than you earn – that's the foundation. Not by being a miser and never enjoying anything. But by being intentional about your spending. By understanding your values and spending money on things that matter to you and cutting out the stuff that doesn't.

Then, once you're saving money, the question becomes: What do you do with it?

If you just keep it in a regular savings account earning 0.01% interest, inflation is eating away at your purchasing power. You're losing money in real terms, even though the number looks the same.

If you invest it, you can get returns that actually beat inflation. That's how wealth is built.

Now, investing has risk. That's the whole trade-off. Higher potential returns come with higher risk. Lower risk comes with lower returns. You need to understand your tolerance for risk – how much volatility can you handle, emotionally and practically?

So here's the smart approach: Understand your risk tolerance, then build a portfolio that matches that. A balanced approach that you can stick with.

Here's the other thing: Start early. I know that sounds like every financial advisor ever, but the math of compound interest is incredible. If you invest $100 a month starting at age 25, assuming a 7% annual return, you'd have over $500,000 by age 65. If you wait until age 35 to start, you'd have about $250,000. Same monthly investment, but time made a huge difference. Time is your friend in investing.

So here's my advice: Don't stress if you're not earning a ton right now. Focus on spending less than you earn. Start investing something – even $50 a month – as early as possible. And think long-term. Don't obsess over short-term market movements.

Wealth is usually built slowly. It's not glamorous. It's boring. It's consistent. But it works.

3. **What scares you most about money and finance?** Let's address that.

---

### Key Concepts:

- **Earning:** Income from work or business
- **Spending:** Money you use to live and enjoy life
- **Saving:** Money you're not spending
- **Investing:** Making your saved money work for you
- **Compound interest:** The magical force that builds wealth over time
- **Inflation:** The decrease in purchasing power over time
